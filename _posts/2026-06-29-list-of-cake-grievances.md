<p>My red velvet birthday cake has been demolished by one of my two siblings. Half of it was left (the equivalent of seven servings), and now it is gone.</p>

<p>I’ve talked about this cake a lot to many different people, not because it’s the best cake I’ve ever eaten, but because it’s given way to so many complaints. To begin, I see a red velvet cake in Whole Foods, and I think, “Wow, that’s a pretty nice-looking cake.” It’s a dairy-free, gluten-free cake, because dairy makes me throw up, and maybe people with gluten intolerances also tend to have dairy intolerances. Or maybe it seems healthier? I’m not sure. I don’t care. I wanted a birthday cake, and there it was.</p>

<p>As soon as we get home, my mom tells me to cut the cake. We’re going to do the whole song and dance right here, right now. Oh no, there isn’t a candle; we forgot to get one. Oh, I just found one in a drawer. It’s a very strange candle, with petals that bloom and a sparkler in the middle that does what sparklers do. It spins atop the cake, which is worrying to me, because it’s getting closer and closer to the edge, but it stops before it falls and lights our whole dining room on fire.</p>

<p>My mom yells for my brother to come upstairs and sing Happy Birthday. He doesn’t come. She pulls out her phone and begins recording. She and my dad sing Happy Birthday. It’s a bit off-key, as most happy birthdays are. I want to blow this monstrous candle out, but it’s begun to play the tune of Happy Birthday, and it won’t stop. It really won’t stop. When they have finished their rendition of the song, I blow out the candles and make my wish, my eyes shut as tight as they can close. The candle is still playing its synthetic Happy Birthday drone.</p>

<p>My mom fetches a large knife, and I try to shut off the candle. There is no switch. I deconstruct the base, and the music gets louder. I pull it apart, piece by piece, and I get to the little microchip speaker inside, with a small button-cell battery at the base and a copper wire twisted to close the circuit. Even now, it’s still playing. If I let the two thin copper segments in the middle touch the base, it plays it again. If I pull them up and away, it goes quiet. I pull them up and away, then slip to my room to add the little thing to my trinkets.</p>

<p>I like this cake. I like the red velvet layers and the sweet something-like-cream-cheese frosting. I like the way it slices underneath my fork and the way it feels when I bite down. The day after my birthday, I leave to go visit the college I’ll be attending in the fall. It’s beautiful, it’s new, and the food tastes great over there. I see cows, horses, and chickens. There are stands for fresh milk by the side of the road, then fresh eggs. The air feels different, the roads are different, and I feel like I can breathe a little deeper. I come back on Sunday. I have a little bit of cake. I wake up on Monday feeling a little bit anxious. There are new, hard things to do, like my first day of therapy. I want to cry more than half of the time, and she is very gracious in not mentioning how choked-up I sound. My housing assignment comes out, and my roommate seems wonderful. I wake up wanting cake. When the reverberations of trying to be kinder to myself stop knocking me over, I go downstairs, and I cut a slice of cake.</p>

<p>It’s my sister’s birthday on this Monday, and she is not home. I don’t blame her, and I’m no better—I left home the day after my birthday. We were born almost four years apart, but those two full days between our birthdays cuts it just short of four. I ask my mom to take me driving, which we only do in a very large office parking lot. I drive around and around, and then we go to get my sister’s birthday cake. First, we stop by Barnes and Noble. I love Barnes and Noble. As I walk along the shelves, the stack of books in my arms grows, but at the end, I choose just one. It’s Flowers for Algernon, and the cute little rat on the cover has drawn me in. We go to get a cake.</p>

<p>When we get home, I help put away groceries and make myself dinner. After dinner, I really want a slice of my red velvet cake. I’ve been thinking about it since I saw a book about cake in Barnes and Noble. I go downstairs, and it’s not there. I sort through the fridge, thinking maybe someone has moved it, or maybe it’s somehow been relocated to another fridge. It hasn’t. I go upstairs and ask my mom if she moved my cake. She says she saw the empty container in the recycling, and I am in disbelief. This morning, after I took my reward slice, there was half the cake left. It is all gone.</p>

<p>If you get a red velvet cake to cure the birthday blues, make sure you keep it somewhere safe.</p>
