<p>
The book I'm reading for English is 274 pages long, excluding the postscript and acknowledgments. For some reason, I have a feeling I won't be able to get away with not reading those last few pages, because I never want to finish a good book. Or maybe I just don't want to let a good book go.
</p>
<p>
There are many books I remember reading and enjoying, and promptly placing them in a basket full of books that might be read or may have already been read. There are very few I remember liking so much that I decided that every last word required my full attention. I get to the last page of a book so often, and it always takes me by surprise. I can feel the weight shifting from my right hand to the left, I can feel the spine slowly unbending in relief, and I can feel my heart swelling in my chest, my breath catching in some space between my lungs and mouth. I read that last page without letting each inked letter stain my eyes, and I regret it every time. I swivel toward the next page and find that I've stumbled to the end, and I feel lost. I reread the page because I was given this ending, and so I need it to be cemented forever as the ending. I slow myself down with a finger under each line, sometimes mouthing the words to make sure it's all there. I want to make sure it's all there. I used to read quickly, but I find I am so very slow now. It's devastating. 
</p>
<p>
Anyway, the book is <i>The Anthropocene Reviewed</i> by John Green. In the introduction, he references so many different people and their ideas, which I suppose comes naturally when you absorb so much information and regurgitate it for others. He writes that he wants to pay attention to what he pays attention to, a reference to Amy Krouse Rosenthal's work. He writes that it has taken him all his life to fall in love with the world, and I find myself wondering how that can happen. How do you fall in love with the world? He wants to live his life.
</p>
<p>
I started writing this because I want to talk about some flowers that I saw. There was a day, and it was recent, when I felt such a strong urge to walk and keep walking and never stop that I simply had to walk. I didn't walk forever, but I can't say I have zero regret about stopping. I walked. I kept going until I felt like the clumsy flow of air through my respiratory system had found some sort of pattern to follow. I wish I had kept going. Never mind, I saw these beautiful flowers when I walked. They were these tiny blueish-purple flowers, maybe indigo, that were so vibrant, so bright, that I couldn't even believe they were real. I saw them, and I didn't believe what I saw. They didn't look like they belonged against the dusty green of a slowly reviving lawn. Nothing beautiful seems to belong on a boring, American lawn. 
</p>
<p>
I wish I could describe them. I wish I could paint a lovely picture and say exactly what I saw, but I don't even know what I saw. They were so colorful, they looked more like the absence of color filled in with light. They looked like ultramarine spots in my vision, so vivid they had to have some source of light, maybe a sun, at their core. I want to see them again.
</p>
