<p>
When everything starts to sound the same, and nothing really does anything at all to move me forward or hold me still, I listen to NPR Tiny Desk Concerts on YouTube.
</p>
<p>
I grew up listening to Kiss 108, Boston’s #1 Hit Music Station on iHeartRadio, which was one of two radio stations my dad would tune in to as he drove me to school in the mornings. The other was WBZ News, but it was so easy to drift back into cold morning sleep when they started talking about whatever it was they talked about. 
</p>
<p>
I almost forgot, but I also listened to Magic 106.7, which always told me to ‘Relax and Unwind,’ even as the hosts blasted a new Justin Bieber song.
</p>
<p>
Do you know anything about the magic of radio station music? Radio stations are lovely because you have no choice in what they play unless you call in and request a song, so you either let the sound wash over you or you turn the volume knob all the way to the right and wait until they play the next song. And there’s always a next song, isn’t that wonderful? I learned a strong Boston pride that has almost entirely dissipated, but these pop radio stations with their sparkly music fizzing like an Alka-Seltzer in water stuck with me. The proof lies in the fact that I used to write “Boston, MA,” whenever a document asked me to list the city and state I live in. I do not live in Boston, nor have I ever. With all due love, hopefully, I never will.
</p>
<p>
At some point, when I was allowed mostly unsupervised access to the computer in the living room, I discovered the wonders that YouTube had to offer. After five videos of women with beautiful rainbow hair showing me how to make edible gluesticks and sneak snacks into class, I stumbled upon the greatest joy YouTube could give me: lyric videos. Among my first liked songs are the lyric videos for Breathe by Olly Alexander, Rather Be covered by Pentatonix, and Buzzcut Season by Lorde. I sat in front of the too-bright screen and sang along. I sang along to any lyric video I could find, and I sang along even if I didn’t know the melody. All I needed was the block of text on a sunset-colored background.
</p>
<p>
I soon added music videos to my repertoire. I sat there silently, my eyes wide open in fear of missing any little thing. Everything is so bright, so brilliant, so dazzlingly new. I want to let all the light enter my eyes. I want to let the stars fall into my pupils and shrink them to tiny black dots. Along with these shiny new items came a broader range of types of music I was consuming. In the glimmering summery pop of Kiss 108 mornings, it didn’t occur to me that people could make sad music. I’d heard worship songs during Catholic mass, but there was never a hint of defeat. Sad endings were out of the question. As I began to struggle with my own feelings and let them tear me to shreds, sad music was there to replicate the tightening of muscles and the catching of breath between the walls of a throat closing around a thready pulse.
</p>
<p>
Years passed, and with the slim cold rectangle of my brand-new phone pressed against my hip, I betrayed YouTube for Pandora. I accrued a solid selection of over a hundred songs before my middle school crush told me they didn’t have more than thirty songs in their liked songs playlist because hearting them meant they had to be <em>super special</em>. When I switched from Pandora to Spotify, I was careful to only add songs that had that ‘special’ quality. Now, I heart every song that I like, which has totaled to over 600, which is really just chump change. 
<p>
For a long time, I only listened to sad music. Not only because it felt better to slip my head under the warm, comforting waters of despair, but also because forcing myself to listen to happy music felt like gasping for fresh air and instead filling my lungs with cloyingly sweet perfume. I choked on my attempts to be happier. I love the melancholy, but I’ve been known to deny it. I don’t want people to know I listen to sad music, because when you listen to sad music with other people, they either say, “wow, that’s a sad song,” or “I’ve never been in more pain.” An easy way out is to just say you like it. 
</p>
<p>
I’ve wanted to be a good singer since I was seven years old. When Magic 106.7 or Kiss 108 played a song I had half-memorized the lyrics to, I would sing along. To be clear, I first asked if I could sing along. I asked if I could sing.
</p>
<p>
One day, when I was very, very young, while sitting in the carpool line waiting for my sister to get out of school, I asked my dad if I could sing. The radio wasn’t on, and the only sound was the AC breathing hot, stale air from the front of the car to the back, and what I sang was a Christmas song. I didn’t know many songs at that point, but I knew I wanted to sing.
</p>
<p>
Everything is so clean on Spotify. The sound waves that enter my ear canals are so crisp, so smooth, so light, and so perfect. The equalizer on my earbuds is set to Vocal, which dims everything but the singer’s voice. It’s terribly intimate, just listening to someone sing, even more so when I have noise canceling turned on and nothing enters other than a voice or two. Singing is an exhalation of breath given up to the sound. If I need some distance, I turn the equalizer to Treble or Bass Boost. Sometimes, the sound quality is just too good. It doesn’t sound like a real problem to have, music sounding too good, but it’s unbearable at times. I turn to YouTube for crappier sound quality.
</p>
<p>
The first time I heard a NPR Tiny Desk Concert was not on YouTube, even though that’s where they live. I listened to a snippet of Pool by Samia, recorded live on NPR, and it devastated me. Spotify’s high quality didn’t really do it justice. I was devastated, sure, but on a much smaller scale, and barely comparable to what I’d felt while scrolling Instagram reels. I opened YouTube and looked for the video, and I found it just to devastate myself some more. 
</p>
<p>
Most of the time, time seems to move with cruel efficiency. There is always, always another thing to do, another assignment or task or self-care item waiting to be checked off, and that should be fine, but the speed at which everything is coming at me is unsustainable. When I’m surrounded by people who want to do <em>more</em>, I feel a bit sick. I console myself with the fact that I am still able to feel rage and indignation, that there is still a fragment of stubborn little girl surviving within me. I don’t want to do more. I mean, I do, but only because everyone seems to want that. I don’t actually want that. I want to do less. Anyways, despite the never-ending, forever-accumulating mountain of action items to sift through, some days are excruciatingly slow. Nothing does anything at all for me, not Instagram reels, not YouTube shorts, not reading, not writing, not working, not anything or everything. I can’t lie on the floor or in my bed. I just can’t. I can’t even watch my favorite YouTubers train for a 5K or try geocaching on roller blades. Instead, I listen to NPR Tiny Desk Concerts.
</p>
<p>
You can hear every breath they take, the adjustment of deft fingers on frets, the tapping of someone’s foot, the effort it takes to press down ivory keys. There is laughter in between songs, applause from shifting bodies, a drummer counting off, and a light introduction before the next. And there is always a next, until they come to a close. Then, I find a new artist’s Tiny Desk Concert.
</p>
<p>
I like that the space they perform in is so small, so intimate. It really is Tiny. I like that they are protected by the wooden Desk in front of them, which is littered with dozens of books and various trinkets. I like that they can cause so much pain and feel so much hurt in such a small space. The top comment on Samia’s NPR Tiny Desk debut reads “tiny desk of agony and despair,” and it’s funny, but it aches. The sound is smaller, shrinking itself down to condense the hurt in a supercharged cube of everything painful spread across twenty minutes.
</p>
<p>
NPR Tiny Desk Concerts are sacred. They are hallowed. I do not want to listen to them with anyone other than myself. I do not want to share this music with someone, and I do not want to cheapen what NPR Tiny Desk Concerts has saved me from. In my world, there is this beautiful pool of pure ache, and I don’t want to wade in it, but I don’t want people to run splashing through it and leave midnight footprints all over the grass. Even if they're people I love. So, NPR Tiny Desk is just for me, and I’ll share it only with the people in the comment section. I agree, this is the tiny desk of agony and despair. I love it.
</p>
